> [!important] **Enduring understanding**: The goal of this module is to use your reading comprehension skills to understand texts about the complex and rewarding relationships between friends and roommates. Living with other people is a common experience that requires communication and understanding. By analyzing texts on this topic, you will build the vocabulary and strategic reading skills necessary to navigate these social situations in an English-speaking context.

> [!important] **Essential Question**: How do our relationships with friends and roommates teach us about communication, compromise, and community?

> [!important] **Reading**: [[3) Sharing Spaces- Friends and Roommates]]

> [!important] **Learning outcomes**
> 
> : Friends and Roommates
> 
> - Identify the main idea in texts describing friendships and roommate situations.
> - Locate specific details about people's personalities, habits, and house rules.
> - Determine the meaning of new vocabulary related to social life and cohabitation from context.
> - Use key vocabulary and grammar to describe a friend or an ideal roommate.

        Experiments have shown that larvae placed in chambers with recordings of thriving reefs swim toward the speakers, while those exposed to recordings from degraded reefs show weaker responses.         During China‚Äôs Song dynasty (960‚Äì1279), merchants in several cities began using paper instruments in place of bulky bronze coins. These privately issued notes, called¬†_jiaozi_, were originally receipts that certified a deposit of coin at a money‚Äëchanger‚Äôs shop.         As the scale of commerce grew, the government moved to regulate the practice. Eventually, the state monopolized note production and created official paper currency backed by stores of coin and goods.     - Reading 3 - The Printing Press and Pamphlet Culture
        
        When movable‚Äëtype printing appeared in mid‚Äëfifteenth‚Äëcentury Europe, its most visible products were not massive books but small, quickly produced pamphlets. Cheap sheets, folded once or twice, could be printed overnight and carried by peddlers along trade routes that already knit towns together. Because pamphlets were brief and topical, they became a vehicle for everything from sensational news to practical advice and religious argument.
        
        Authorities worried about the social consequences of this new speed of communication. Some cities licensed presses and required prior approval for controversial works; others allowed publication first and punished printers later. In either case, censorship proved uneven. Copies slipped across borders, and reprints sprang up wherever sympathetic buyers could be found. Meanwhile, literacy was rising‚Äîslowly and unevenly‚Äîcreating wider audiences without eliminating the need for public readings in marketplaces and churches.
        
        Pamphlet culture did not replace learned scholarship, but it changed the rhythm of public debate. Brief formats encouraged sharp claims and memorable slogans. The same features that helped ideas spread‚Äîlow cost, portability, and speed‚Äîalso made it difficult to ensure accuracy. Yet even critics acknowledged that the press created a new sense of simultaneity: people in distant towns could argue about the same text within days.
